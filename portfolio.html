Proposed Solution Design

The Ergonomic Crisis

Long hours of gaming and studying at a desk put sustained pressure on the wrist and hand. Repetitive strain injuries such as carpal tunnel syndrome and de Quervain’s tenosynovitis are linked to constant clicking, typing and restricted wrist movement. Our research highlights that most existing interventions, such as ergonomic chairs, posture videos and wrist supports, rely heavily on user discipline and rarely provide real-time feedback.

  • Carpal tunnel syndrome from median nerve compression
  • Tendonitis from repetitive mouse and keyboard use
  • Lack of real-time, preventative ergonomic guidance

Concept Features

We designed a smart wrist-relief glove and companion mobile interface that visualises wrist strain in real time, recommends recovery exercises, and explores how AI can support long-term wrist health.

Prototype: AI Tactical Support

The companion app explores how an AI “Field Medic” could analyse wrist-strain data and user reports to deliver personalised ergonomic advice. In our mockup, the AI coach generates post-session debriefs and short, actionable tips about wrist posture, micro-breaks and recovery strategy.

Concept: Bio-Feedback Gauge

In the final prototype, a colour-coded gauge visualises wrist strain severity (green, amber, red) and highlights when strain exceeds a critical 75% threshold. The interface also handles error states such as “No signal” from the glove, offering Retry and Help options so users know how to respond.

Process & Iteration

1. User Research & Personas

We conducted early research with gamers and students who regularly spend long hours at the computer. A recurring theme was that people tend to ignore early wrist discomfort, and many described existing health apps as too “clinical” or boring. This led us to define our primary persona as a performance-driven gamer who cares about winning first, but still needs subtle protection against long-term injury.

Key Finding

Gamers prefer an interface that feels like part of their gaming rig instead of a clinical health app.

2. Low-Fidelity Testing

Our first paper prototype used four tabs – Monitor, Recovery, AI Coach and Data. We evaluated it using Nielsen’s Usability Heuristics and discovered a problem with “Match between system and real world”: medical labels and jargon made the interface feel like a clinic app rather than part of a gaming setup. Based on this, we switched to clearer, game-inspired language and simplified the hierarchy.

3. Hi-Fi Prototype Testing

We ran three rounds of usability testing with our interactive mockup and calculated System Usability Scale (SUS) scores of 77.5, 62.5 and 75.0 across different versions. Feedback led us to clarify the strain gauge (adding the “Strain Load” label and colour logic), introduce a cumulative load slider, expand recovery content, and add warm-up protocols plus educational tips about the dangers of wrist over-straining.

Reflection & Future Impact

User Feedback on Concept

Our concept testing successfully demonstrated that medical advice can be gamified. Our concept testing showed that a game-styled interface can make ergonomic guidance feel less clinical and more engaging. Across three usability tests, SUS scores ranging from 62.5 to 77.5 indicated above-average usability with clear improvements between versions. Participants especially valued the live strain monitoring and the way the gauge visually signalled when to take a break.

"This is the first time I actually stopped playing to stretch because the red gauge scared me. Even as a mockup, it feels effective."

- User #4

Design Challenges

  • 1

    Data Simulation vs. Reality

    We did not have access to an actual sensor-equipped glove, so we simulated wrist-strain data with a logic-based model. This allowed us to test the interface and feedback, but our findings focus on usability rather than medical accuracy.

  • 2

    Human-Centered AI

    Designing the AI “Field Medic” was challenging because it needed to feel like part of a gaming setup, not a clinical app. We iterated on the tone and wording to make the messages short, empathetic and tactical, while avoiding medical diagnoses.
